The Forecast Fork: CBO’s Trend, or Your Own Assumptions

The forecast was hiding in plain sight

On the last few pages we built up potential GDP piece by piece: labor, capital, and the productivity leftover called TFP. Everything we did was about history: what the economy could have produced from 1949 up through the most recent data.

So where does the forecast come from? You might expect a separate crystal ball, some extra model that takes over once history runs out. There isn’t one.

Remember those smooth trend lines we drew through the bumpy historical data? To make a forecast, the model just keeps drawing the same line past the edge of the data. That’s it.

Note 1: Extrapolation

Extrapolation means continuing a pattern past the data you actually have. If your height grew about two inches a year and you want to guess next year’s height, you draw the same slope one year further. The forecast in this model is exactly that: the historical trend lines, extended forward.

So when CBO “forecasts” potential TFP or potential employment for years where the data is thin or missing, it is really just trusting the slope it already measured and walking it forward. The trend that smooths history is the same trend that predicts the future.

The fork we added: trust CBO, or speak for yourself

That raises an obvious teaching question. What if you don’t believe CBO’s extended trend? Maybe you think American productivity will stay sluggish, or that an aging population will drag down how fast the workforce grows. The published model gives you no knob to turn.

So we added one. Think of it like a fork in a road. For each of two key series, TFP (the productivity leftover) and employment (how many people are working), you reach a boundary year and choose a path:

The default boundary is the first quarter of 2010, written 2010Q1 in the code. Everything before it is real historical estimation and never changes.
  • "cbo": keep going straight. Trust CBO’s extended trend exactly as before.
  • "user": take the off-ramp. Splice in your own assumed future trend instead (for example, “assume TFP grows only 0.5% per year from here on”).

The two forks are set independently, so you can second-guess productivity while still trusting CBO on jobs, or the other way around.

Note 2: Splice

A splice joins your assumed future onto the real historical estimate at the boundary year. Picture two pieces of rope tied together so there’s no gap: we keep CBO’s rope up to the boundary, then attach your rope from the boundary onward, tied to the last real value so the line never jumps. The history before the seam stays byte-for-byte identical to CBO’s.

Growing your assumption, one quarter at a time

Say you tell the model “assume TFP grows 0.5% per year from 2010 on.” The code grabs the last real value just before the boundary (the anchor) and grows it forward, quarter by quarter, at your rate. Because the data is quarterly, a yearly rate gets converted into the equivalent quarterly nudge before it’s applied (one year is four quarters, so the quarterly step is the fourth root of the annual growth).

Turning 0.5% per year into a quarterly step: (1 + 0.5/100) ** 0.25 - 1. Compounding that four times gets you back to 0.5% over the full year.

Here is the heart of it, copied straight from forecast.py. The loop walks across the projection window, and each quarter is just the previous quarter nudged up by your growth rate:

def splice_growth(cbo_series, growth_annual_pct, boundary):
    boundary_p, window = _projection_window(cbo_series.index, boundary)
    anchor = boundary_p - 1
    if anchor not in cbo_series.index:
        raise ValueError(
            f"Cannot splice: anchor quarter {anchor} (just before "
            f"FORECAST_START={boundary}) is not in the series."
        )

    g = growth_annual_pct.reindex(window).ffill().bfill()

    out = cbo_series.copy()
    prev = anchor
    for q in window:
        q_growth = (1 + g.loc[q] / 100) ** 0.25 - 1
        out.loc[q] = out.loc[prev] * (1 + q_growth)
        prev = q
    return out

Read it like a recipe. Start at the anchor (the last real value). For each quarter q in the window, take the previous quarter out.loc[prev], multiply by (1 + q_growth), and store the result. Then slide forward: this quarter becomes the prev for the next. By anchoring to the last real value, the spliced path starts exactly where history left off, so there’s no jump at the seam.

The “off switch” is the whole point

Now here’s the part that makes this safe to ship. The override only ever runs if you ask for it. In TFP’s case, that decision lives in one tiny function in tfp.py:

def project_potential_tfp(iprodfe_cbo, forecast_mode, user_trend_file,
                          forecast_start):
    if forecast_mode == "cbo":
        return iprodfe_cbo
    if forecast_mode == "user":
        return apply_user_forecast(
            iprodfe_cbo, user_trend_file, forecast_start
        ).rename("iprodfe")
    raise ValueError(
        f"TFP_FORECAST_MODE must be 'cbo' or 'user', got {forecast_mode!r}"
    )

Look at the first two lines of the body. In "cbo" mode the function hands back iprodfe_cbo, the trend path, completely untouched and immediately returns. The splicing code below never even runs.

That is why the default model still reproduces CBO’s published numbers exactly. The “what if” machinery is there, but in plain CBO mode it’s like a light switch left in the off position: present, wired up, drawing no power. You only flip it on when you want to.

Same history, four different futures

Figure 1 shows what happens when you do flip it on. It overlays four runs of the full model: the same engine, fed different assumptions.

The four shipped scenarios: cbo (trust both trends), lowtfp (0.5%/yr TFP), lowemp (0.3%/yr employment), and lowboth (both headwinds at once).

Notice the shape. Through 2009, all four lines sit exactly on top of each other. You can’t tell them apart, because before the boundary every scenario is the identical CBO history. Then, right at 2010, they fan out like fingers spreading from a fist.

One path assumes weaker productivity. Another assumes a slower-growing labor force. A third assumes both headwinds at once. Each pessimistic assumption bends the future a little lower, and together they pull average potential-GDP growth over 2010-2016 from about 1.5% per year (the CBO trend) down toward about 1.1% per year in the gloomiest scenario.

To make that concrete: 1.5% versus 1.1% sounds tiny, but it’s the difference between an economy that roughly doubles in size every 47 years and one that takes about 63. Compounded over a working lifetime, that’s a noticeably poorer country.

Try your own assumption

You don’t have to take our scenarios. Writing your own is meant to be a few lines in a spreadsheet. The model reads a small CSV: a plain table with a date column and exactly one value column:

  • a quarter column, like 2010Q1 (a bare year like 2012 is automatically expanded to all four of its quarters), and
  • either a growth_annual_pct column (your assumed yearly growth rate, in percent) or a level column (the actual value you want to pin), but never both.

Any quarters you leave out simply inherit the last value you gave, so a single row is enough to set a flat assumption for the entire future. The shipped TFP example is two lines long:

quarter,growth_annual_pct
2010Q1,0.5

That one row says: from 2010 on, assume potential productivity grows just 0.5% per year, below CBO’s extended trend. Change the 0.5 to your own number, rerun, and the model rebuilds the whole future around your guess.

A sandbox for the future

This is what turns the project from a finished report into a playground. The hard part (translating CBO’s machinery faithfully) is done and locked. On top of it you now have a single knob for asking grown-up questions about America’s economic future: What if the productivity slowdown is permanent? What if an aging country keeps the labor force flat? You change one number and watch potential GDP respond.

But a sandbox is only worth playing in if the foundation is solid. A model is only trustworthy if it actually reproduces what it claims to, and that’s precisely the promise we make good on next, where we check the Python against CBO’s own published numbers, cell by cell.
